I'm a full-stack engineer at AT&T's Chief Data Office. I build production AI systems — orchestration platforms, agent tooling, document-ingestion pipelines — inside a company where security, compliance, and stakeholder management are not optional.

The thing I care about is the combination. I live at the front of the AI-tooling curve: early MCP adopter, design partner for AT&T's internal AI coding tool, part of a ~25-person design-partner cohort at Windsurf, and the go-to person for AI-fueled coding across the CDO. But I do that *while* shipping real work at enterprise scale. Most people I meet have one side or the other. I'm trying to be good at both, and this blog is where I write about what that's taught me.

A few things I've built or helped build at AT&T:

- **AndiSense** — an AI document-analysis platform I architected and delivered as sole developer, replacing a commercial vendor and saving $3M+ annually. AT&T's CTO highlighted it on the company's 150th-anniversary livestream as "the kind of execution AT&T should look for in future leaders."
- **NAAP** — a data-analytics platform I rebuilt from scratch; it delivered $52M+ in reported business benefits as the backbone of my team's data pipeline.
- **Production AI-coding archetypes** — four merged into AT&T's internal archetype system, covering git-history secret remediation, parallel agents via git worktrees, automated Playwright demos, and a meta-archetype for building more.

Outside work I build a lot of small things to keep my intuitions sharp — a [hybrid quantum-classical starter kit](https://github.com/djmorgan26), AI finance tooling, a text-to-3D-print marketplace, prediction-market engines. The breadth is deliberate; it's how I learn what these tools can and can't do.
